Summary

Two years after his wife's unexpected death, a renowned stage actor and director receives an offer to direct a production of Uncle Vanya in Hiroshima. There, he begins to face the mysteries his wife left behind, with the help of a reserved young woman assigned to be his chauffeur. As they spend time together, secrets from the past and heartfelt confessions emerge.

Critical Review

9.4/10

A Profound Meditation on Loss and Art

Drive My Car is a masterfully crafted exploration of grief, connection, and the healing power of art. Ryusuke Hamaguchi's direction shows remarkable restraint and sensitivity, allowing the complex emotional landscape to unfold naturally over its three-hour runtime.

The film's strength lies in its patient storytelling and rich character development. Hidetoshi Nishijima delivers a powerfully understated performance as Yusuke Kafuku, while Toko Miura brings quiet intensity to the role of Misaki. Their gradually evolving relationship forms the emotional core of the film.

Through its parallel narratives of theatrical production and personal healing, the film creates a profound meditation on how art can help us process trauma and forge meaningful connections. The recurring motif of the red Saab 900 serves as both a physical space for confession and a metaphor for life's journey.
